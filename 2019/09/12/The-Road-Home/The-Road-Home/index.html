###Analysis of the film The Road Home by Zhang Yimou Part I

#Illusion and Reality

  The reference to multiple temporalities and various perceptions of reality in The Road Home may be understood as an allusion to an ideological concern often underlying Zhang Yimou’s films: by alternating temporalities of past and future, Zhang distinguishes illusion of storytelling from historical reality, from where audiences can gain a sight of “history from below”.

   The relation of past, present and future is implicit and intertwined. Based on the story structure of the whole film, the present is shown in the color of black and white, while the story of “my father and my mother” that happened in the past is colorized. The sequence which has vivid colors can be reckoned as the story part in the past. But within the sequence, it can be further divided into past, present and future. Formal elements denote each of the temporalities: the past is from a close-up long take of Di the female protagonist’s face to her running on the road, shown in a slow motion with non-diegetic sound. The present starts from diegetic sounds of her footsteps and breath to the long shot of their reunion. The future starts from the non-diegetic voice of the narrator to the end of the sequence, including a pillow shot and a tilt down shot.

  These formal elements affect how we perceive the different temporalities. The close-up of her face conveys the intangible past as an illusion, which is created from her dream and shown in the narrative. The first few scenes of her blurred, dull eyes, slow movement and her murmuring show that she is in the state of half-awake, and her surrounding may be an illusion from her dream. Her weakness and unconsciousness imply her illusive thought that she hears the voice of Mr. Luo the male protagonist. By using the close-up, Zhang shortens the distance between audiences and Di, and audiences are more likely to feel the same illusion that Di conveys. In addition, the following close-up emphasizes her facial expression to express her strong emotion to audiences. She does not wake up from illusion until she hears the fact that Mr. Luo is back for her. Tears burst out from her eyes, falling down her cheek, and her eyes are bright again. The tears show her grievance, delight, relief, and she cannot wait to see him. From the close-up long take, audiences follow the emotional transition from illusion, uncertainty on reality to the satisfaction and relief.

  The long take of Di standing with the crowds by the front gate of the school makes the present seem realistic. The long take shows that before Di arrives, there are already crowds gathering at the school to see Mr. Luo back to teach again. The crowds are the witnesses of their reunion. Since they exist in the present, the witnesses add credibility to the story told by the narrator, “me”. The long shot and tilt down shot make the future seem implicit and continuous. The pillow shot of landscape—no people in this shot, only a road to the town covered by white snow—implies the transition of the story, which is a final reunion after a two-year separation. The shot also leaves audience to imagine their life after that. Audiences can have a glimpse of the future from the narrative which comes to the end, and thus the story of the future is implicit and unclear. However, audiences already know the present from the beginning of the film is that “my father” passed away, and they can make assumptions to the life of the family after that. Therefore, the future is also continuous.
